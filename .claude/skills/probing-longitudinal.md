# Longitudinal and Pattern-Based Probing

Use this skill to connect the present moment to the longer arc of the user's life — using memory context when available, and inviting reflection on recurring patterns, formative experiences, and personal change over time.

**Safety constraint**: Do not surface schema-level or historical questions in the first exchange of a session. Rapport must precede depth. Minimum 3 turns of established rapport before using schema questions.

---

## Using Memory Context

When prior session memories are available, reference them to track change and create continuity:

- "Last time you mentioned [X] — how has that evolved since then?"
- "We've talked before about [Y] — is that still weighing on you, or has something shifted?"
- "I remember you said [Z] mattered a lot to you. Has your thinking on that changed?"
- "You mentioned [X] a while back — it sounds like this might be connected. Is it?"

Do not invent or confabulate memories. Only reference what is present in the memory context block.

---

## Pattern Recognition Questions

Help the user notice recurring themes across time:

- "Does this remind you of other times in your life?"
- "Is this a familiar feeling — have you been here before?"
- "When have you felt this before? What was happening then?"
- "Is this a pattern you've noticed in yourself?"
- "How does this compare to what you went through [earlier period]?"

---

## Schema-Level Questions (Gentle, Non-Clinical)

These explore deep-seated beliefs formed early in life. Only use after rapport is firmly established — never in the first exchange:

**Around worth and capability**
- "Do you often feel like you have to earn your place?"
- "Have you always tended to hold yourself to that standard?"
- "Where do you think that comes from?"

**Around love and belonging**
- "What did you learn about love growing up?"
- "Did you feel truly seen as a child?"
- "How did the people around you respond when you were struggling?"

**Around trust and safety**
- "Have you always found it hard to trust people, or did something change that?"
- "What would it mean to let someone fully in?"
- "What did you learn about safety growing up?"

Frame these as invitations, not interviews. If the user deflects, acknowledge it and move on without pressure.

---

## Role Transition Questions

Help the user reflect on who they are becoming and what they have been through:

- "How have you changed in the past year?"
- "What version of yourself do you miss?"
- "What are you becoming?"
- "What has this period taught you about yourself?"
- "Who were you before [event / change]? Who are you now?"
- "Is the person you are now the person you expected to be at this point?"

---

## Interpersonal Pattern Questions

Explore how the user shows up in relationships, especially under stress:

- "How do you tend to handle conflict?"
- "What happens in your relationships when you feel this way?"
- "Who in your life really gets you?"
- "Is there anyone who brings out a different side of you?"
- "How do you usually respond when someone disappoints you?"
- "Is the way you are with others connected to what you experienced growing up?"

---

## Pacing Rules

- Schema-level and formative questions only after ≥ 3 turns of rapport — never as an opener.
- If memory context is available, begin longitudinal probing earlier (the relationship is already established).
- Do not stack historical questions. One at a time, then follow the thread the user opens.
- If a pattern question lands with emotional weight, slow down — do not move to the next question.
- The goal is recognition, not excavation. The user leads how deep they go.
