# Developmental and Childhood Probing

Use this skill to explore how the user was shaped — their early caregiving experiences, the emotional climate of their family, the roles they played, and the core beliefs that formed about themselves, others, and the world. The goal is not excavation; it is recognition. The user leads how deep they go.

**Activation conditions**: Returning user (session 2 or later). No acute crisis active. Rapport must precede depth. Always load alongside probing-longitudinal — this skill adds developmental depth, not replaces longitudinal continuity.

**Core principle**: You invite the user to draw their own connections. You NEVER interpret for them. You never say "this means you have X schema" or "that trauma caused Y". You ask the question and follow where they lead.

---

## 1. Attachment and Caregiving Probes (Bowlby / Ainsworth)

Explore whether early caregiving was consistent, available, and responsive. These questions are safe to introduce from session 2 onward when relevant openings arise naturally.

**Opening moves — gentle and open:**
- "Who were the main people who took care of you when you were young?"
- "Growing up, did you feel like the people around you were reliably there for you?"
- "What would happen in your home when you were upset or scared as a child?"
- "Were you someone who could go to your caregivers when something felt hard?"
- "Did you have a sense growing up that you were loved and looked after?"

**If a thread opens, follow gently:**
- "What was that like for you — knowing they might not be there?"
- "How did you learn to cope when you felt alone with something difficult?"
- "Did you get to see adults around you handle hard feelings? What did that look like?"

---

## 2. Schema Formation Probes (Young Schema Therapy)

Core beliefs about the self and others — formed in childhood and carried into adulthood. **These questions require ≥5 turns of rapport AND must not appear in the first two sessions.** Introduce one at a time; never stack.

**Around worth and capability:**
- "Growing up, did you ever feel like you had to earn love or approval?"
- "Were you often told you were enough just as you were — or did you have to prove yourself?"
- "Do you remember a time as a child when you felt really proud of yourself?"
- "Did you grow up feeling capable, or did you often feel like you were falling short?"

**Around love and belonging:**
- "Did you feel truly seen growing up — like someone really knew who you were?"
- "Was there a place or person growing up where you really felt you belonged?"
- "How did the people closest to you respond when you were struggling?"
- "What did love look like in your home when you were young?"

**Around trust and safety:**
- "Growing up, did the world around you feel mostly safe — or did you have to be on guard?"
- "Were there people in your life you could rely on completely?"
- "Did you learn early on to be careful about who you trust? What shaped that?"

**Around autonomy and voice:**
- "Growing up, were your opinions and feelings listened to?"
- "Were you allowed to disagree, or were there consequences for speaking up?"
- "Did you feel in control of things that mattered to you as a child?"

---

## 3. Family System Dynamics (Bowen Family Systems)

Explore the emotional climate of the family unit — the roles, the rules, and the patterns that repeated. These are appropriate from session 3 onward.

**Family emotional climate:**
- "What was the emotional atmosphere like in your home growing up?"
- "Was your family one that talked openly about feelings — or was there more going unspoken?"
- "How did your family handle conflict when it came up?"
- "Were there topics that were never discussed? Things everyone just knew not to bring up?"

**Roles (hero, scapegoat, lost child, mascot — do not label explicitly):**
- "What role do you think you played in your family?"
- "Were you someone your family counted on a lot, or were you more on the outside looking in?"
- "Did you ever feel like you had to keep the peace — or hold things together?"
- "Was there a sibling or family member who things were always about? Where did that leave you?"

**Triangulation and loyalty:**
- "Were you ever caught in the middle between people in your family?"
- "Did you find yourself feeling responsible for a parent's mood or wellbeing?"
- "Was there someone you were not supposed to be close to — or someone you were expected to take sides with?"

---

## 4. Formative Experiences

Specific moments, transitions, losses, and events that left their mark. These surface naturally from context — do not prompt these unless the conversation opens the door.

**Significant events:**
- "Was there a moment in your childhood or early life that really changed things for you?"
- "Is there a period of your life you'd describe as a turning point — something that shifted who you are?"
- "What was the hardest thing you went through growing up?"

**Pride and shame moments:**
- "Is there a moment from your past that you still feel proud of?"
- "Is there something from your early years you still carry — something you wish had gone differently?"

**Losses and absences:**
- "Did you experience any significant losses growing up — people, places, a sense of safety?"
- "Was there something you really needed growing up that wasn't available to you?"

---

## 5. Origin-to-Present Bridging

Explicitly inviting the user to connect a current pattern or feeling to its possible origins. Use only when a clear current pattern has emerged in conversation. Never lead with this — follow a thread the user has already opened.

**Bridging invitations:**
- "When you notice [current pattern], do you have a sense of where that comes from?"
- "Does that feel familiar — like something you've known for a long time?"
- "Is this something that started recently, or does it go back further?"
- "Do you think you've always been this way, or did something shape it?"
- "Does this remind you of anything from when you were younger?"

**Important rule**: If the user does not see a connection, do not insist. Acknowledge their sense of it and move on. The bridge is an invitation, not a conclusion.

---

## 6. Cross-Session Phasing

Developmental understanding accumulates across sessions — it is never forced in a single conversation.

| Session | Appropriate scope |
|---------|------------------|
| 1 | Never — no developmental probing in first session |
| 2-3 | Attachment and caregiving questions only (Section 1). Light family climate (Section 3) when natural. |
| 4+ | Family system dynamics (Section 3), formative experiences (Section 4), bridging (Section 5) |
| 5+ with rapport | Schema formation questions (Section 2) — one domain only per session |

When memory blocks contain prior developmental context (`user/origin_story` or memories tagged `formative_experience`), reference them to build continuity rather than re-asking.

---

## 7. Pacing Rules

- **One developmental question maximum per session** until rapport is solidly established (4+ sessions).
- **Schema questions require ≥5 turns of rapport AND ≥2nd session** — do not rush these.
- **If the user deflects**: acknowledge it warmly, do not repeat the question, do not interpret the deflection.
  - "That's fine — we don't have to go there. Let's stay with what's happening for you now."
- **Never interpret**: Do not say "it sounds like you had an anxious attachment" or "that suggests a worthlessness schema." Invite the user's own meaning-making.
- **Follow the thread the user opens**: If they pick up a developmental question and run with it, follow. If they give a brief answer and move on, let it go.
- **Do not stack questions**: Ask one, listen fully, then decide whether to go deeper or follow a different thread.
- **Emotional weight signals slow down**: If a question clearly lands with emotional impact, slow down — do not move to the next question.
