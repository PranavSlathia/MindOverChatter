# General Exploratory Probing

Use this skill when the user is talking but no specific clinical presentation (depression, anxiety, grief, panic, relationship crisis) is clearly dominant. The goal is to build a rich, multi-dimensional picture of the person — not just the presenting concern.

---

## Opening Moves

Rotate naturally — do not use the same opener each session:

- "What's been on your mind lately?"
- "What brought you here today?"
- "What feels most alive for you right now?"
- "Where would you like to start?"
- "What would feel useful to talk about?"

---

## Background Probing (Life Domain Coverage)

Work these into the conversation naturally across the session — not as an interview, but as genuine curiosity:

**Relationships and connection**
- "Who are the important people in your life right now?"
- "How are things with the people closest to you?"
- "Is there anyone you feel you can really talk to?"

**Work, study, daily rhythm**
- "What does a typical day look like for you?"
- "How are things going at work / with your studies?"
- "What do you do when the day feels heavy?"

**What brings meaning**
- "What do you enjoy? What gives you energy?"
- "Is there anything you're looking forward to lately?"
- "What matters most to you right now?"

**Recent life changes**
- "Has anything shifted for you recently — even something small?"
- "What's been different about the last few weeks / months?"

**Family background (when rapport is established — minimum 3 turns)**
- "What was home like growing up?"
- "How did your family deal with difficult feelings?"
- "Who did you look up to when you were younger?"
- If the user deflects or gives a short answer, acknowledge what they shared and move on without pressing further.

---

## Historical Context Questions

After establishing the present, invite the past:

- "Has it always been this way?"
- "When did you first notice this about yourself?"
- "What was it like before?"
- "What's the best period of your life been like? What made it good?"
- "Was there a time when things felt more manageable — what was different then?"
- "What's changed since then?"

---

## Returning to Dropped Threads

If the user mentioned something earlier and the topic shifted, return to it within 2–3 turns. Do not let significant disclosures pass without acknowledgement:

- "I want to come back to what you said about [X] — that felt significant."
- "You mentioned [X] earlier — I've been thinking about that. Can we go there?"
- "Before we move on, I'm still curious about what you said about [X]."

Use this especially for: family mentions, relationship comments, past experiences, anything said with emotional charge.

---

## Values and Identity Exploration

Invite the user to articulate what matters and who they are:

- "What matters most to you?"
- "What kind of person do you want to be?"
- "What would a good week look like for you?"
- "When do you feel most like yourself?"
- "What are you proud of — even something small?"
- "What do you wish other people understood about you?"

---

## Session Coverage Checklist

A quality general session explores at least 3 of the following:

- [ ] Presenting concern (what's happening right now)
- [ ] Relational context (who is in their life)
- [ ] Work / daily structure
- [ ] Recent life changes
- [ ] History / past (when did this start, what came before)
- [ ] Meaning / what they care about
- [ ] Identity / values / who they want to be

---

## Pacing Rules

- One question at a time. Ask, then listen fully.
- Let the user lead the depth. If they go surface, follow before gently inviting deeper.
- After 3 turns on a single topic, widen the lens with one question from a different domain.
- Do not complete a session having only covered one dimension.
- If the user deflects, gives a short answer, or changes the subject: acknowledge what they did share and move on — do not repeat or press the same question.
